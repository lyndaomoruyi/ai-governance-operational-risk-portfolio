AI Third-Party Risk Compliance Workbook

The workbook is the main employer-reviewable artifact. It organizes AI use-case inventory, vendor review, control mapping, issue tracking, UAT validation, sources, and a data dictionary in one Excel file.

What it proves

  • Ability to turn an emerging compliance problem into a structured monitoring tool.
  • Practical understanding of risk indicators such as customer impact, data sensitivity, approval status, missing evidence, vendor review, and human oversight.
  • Ability to create audit-ready views instead of leaving compliance data scattered across notes and trackers.

How an employer could use it

A compliance, GRC, IT risk, or vendor management team could use this workbook as a starting point to inventory AI tools, monitor open evidence gaps, track issue ownership, and prepare for internal audit or risk committee review.

D

Synthetic Dataset Included in Workbook

The dataset is built with synthetic AI use cases so no confidential banking, customer, vendor, or employer information is exposed.

Data fields modeled

  • AI use-case ID, AI tool/vendor, business unit, business area, platform type, and model type.
  • Data sensitivity, customer impact, human oversight level, approval status, review status, and evidence completeness.
  • Next review date, risk score, risk tier, control owner, and AI review note.

Why it matters

The dataset supports the same compliance question a bank would ask: which AI-enabled activities are in use, what data is involved, who owns the risk, what evidence is missing, and what requires follow-up?

B

Business Requirements Document

The BRD translates the compliance problem into business need, stakeholder needs, functional requirements, non-functional requirements, and success measures.

Business need

Financial institutions need a practical way to identify, review, monitor, and evidence AI use cases across departments. Compliance teams must be able to see which tools are being used, whether vendors have been reviewed, what data is accessed, whether human oversight exists, and which items require remediation.

Stakeholders

  • Compliance
  • Risk Management
  • IT Security
  • Vendor Management
  • Business Owners
  • Internal Audit
  • Legal / Privacy

Core requirements

  • Capture each AI use case with business area, owner, vendor/tool, data sensitivity, customer impact, and platform type.
  • Assign a risk score and risk tier based on risk indicators and review status.
  • Track missing evidence such as SOC reports, model documentation, data-flow diagrams, vendor due diligence, and human oversight attestations.
  • Map AI governance risks to control objectives, evidence expectations, owners, and review frequency.
  • Provide dashboard-level reporting for high-risk use cases, overdue reviews, missing evidence, and open issues.
U

UAT Test Cases

The UAT section validates that the workflow behaves like a real compliance monitoring process, not just a static dashboard.

Validation scenarios

  • Restricted data: A use case involving restricted/customer-impacting data must be risk-rated High or Critical.
  • Missing evidence: Missing SOC report, model card, or human oversight evidence must trigger a review gap.
  • Overdue review: A review date before the current period must appear as overdue in the dashboard.
  • Human oversight gap: Limited oversight for customer-impacting workflows must increase risk and document a control gap.
  • Vendor-hosted AI: Vendor-hosted SaaS use cases must require third-party due diligence evidence.
  • Issue closure: Closed issues should no longer count as open remediation items.

Why it matters

UAT shows that the project was treated like a business/compliance solution with validation logic, expected outcomes, and pass/fail criteria.

G

AI Governance Validation Note

The governance note explains the responsible AI boundaries of the project and keeps the workflow defensible.

Human review principle

AI-assisted outputs should support compliance review by summarizing risk, highlighting missing evidence, and recommending next actions. Final approval, hold, escalation, or remediation decisions remain with accountable human reviewers.

Data protection

This portfolio uses synthetic data only. It does not include confidential banking records, customer information, employer data, vendor contracts, internal procedures, or regulatory submissions.

Risk boundaries

  • Supports AI use-case inventory and vendor-enabled AI review.
  • Supports evidence tracking, control monitoring, issue remediation, and audit-ready documentation.
  • Does not make credit decisions, replace compliance officers, file regulatory reports, or approve AI use without human review.
S

Sources

The project direction is supported by current AI governance, third-party risk, model risk, and compliance control themes affecting financial services and regulated organizations.

Research basis

  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework — AI governance and risk management structure.
  • NIST AI RMF Core — Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage concepts.
  • FDIC Interagency Guidance on Third-Party Relationships — risk-based third-party review and ongoing monitoring.
  • OCC model risk management guidance — governance, validation, monitoring, controls, and vendor-provided tools.
  • Current banking AI scrutiny themes — data governance, human oversight, vendor oversight, contingency planning, and control readiness.

Why it matters

The project is not built around a generic AI idea. It is tied to practical compliance concerns that teams need to document, monitor, evidence, and explain.
