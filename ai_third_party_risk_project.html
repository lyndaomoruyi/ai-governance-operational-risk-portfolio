Executive Summary

Financial-services teams are adopting AI across compliance, fraud, customer operations, underwriting support, productivity tools, analytics, and vendor-enabled platforms. The risk is not only the AI tool itself; the risk is not knowing who owns it, what data it touches, which third party supports it, what evidence is missing, and whether human oversight is documented.

Industry Context

AI adoption is moving faster than evidence, ownership, and third-party oversight.

This project is positioned around a current financial-services risk area: organizations need a practical way to inventory AI use, classify exposure, review vendor involvement, monitor controls, and prepare evidence before audit or regulatory pressure arrives.

Business problem

AI tools can enter business workflows before teams have a complete inventory, documented owner, data classification, vendor review, approval status, review cadence, or control evidence.

Solution

The workflow centralizes intake, risk scoring, vendor due diligence, control mapping, issue tracking, UAT validation, and stakeholder reporting into one operating view.

Business value

Leaders can see high-risk AI use cases, overdue reviews, missing evidence, limited oversight, and open remediation actions before they become audit or governance issues.

The Problem

Why AI governance breaks down without operational control visibility

The issue is not simply “using AI.” The issue is using AI without clear ownership, vendor evidence, approval status, monitoring cadence, control mapping, human oversight, and escalation logic.

AI enters workflowBusiness teams use internal, vendor, or embedded AI tools.
Evidence is incompleteData terms, SOC reports, subcontractors, privacy review, or approval records may be missing.
Oversight is unclearHuman review rules, override steps, and impact boundaries are not consistently documented.
Controls are not mappedControl owners, review cadence, and remediation steps are not visible in one place.
Audit readiness suffersLeaders lack a clean view of risk, evidence, ownership, and next action.

Before / After Business Value

What changes when AI governance becomes visible

Before: Fragmented AI oversight

  • AI tools are tracked across separate messages, forms, and spreadsheets.
  • Vendor review gaps are not visible to compliance, IT, business, and audit stakeholders.
  • Human oversight requirements may be discussed but not consistently evidenced.
  • Leadership receives late-stage updates instead of a control-ready review queue.

After: Structured governance visibility

  • AI use cases, vendor evidence, risk tier, owner, and review status sit in one workflow.
  • Evidence gaps, overdue reviews, and issue actions are visible before audit pressure.
  • Human oversight is tracked as a control requirement, not an assumption.
  • Stakeholders receive a governance-ready view of risk, ownership, and remediation.
